T3.7: Multi-Script Handwritten Indic Character Recognition
via a Two-Stage CNN Pipeline

Team: Thala_for_a_reason
Abstract. We present a lightweight two-stage convolutional neural network system for recognising handwritten characters across four Indic scripts: Devanagari (46 classes), Tamil (156 classes), Bengali (84 classes), and Telugu (6 vowels). A Script Router—a compact 4-class CNN—identifies the script of an input image; the predicted script gates a dedicated per-script classifier. All five models are trained from scratch on 282,438 training images without any transfer learning. The system achieves a router accuracy of 99.92% and character-level Top-1 accuracies of 99.48% (Devanagari), 97.30% (Tamil), 93.02% (Bengali), and 98.89% (Telugu). End-to-end CPU inference latency is 10.21 ms. We report seven ablation studies, robustness benchmarks under six corruption types, and a live interactive Streamlit demo.

1.  Introduction

Handwritten text digitization is foundational in document analysis, with applications in form processing, educational accessibility, and heritage preservation. Indic scripts are particularly challenging: they are morphologically rich, exhibit high intra-class variability (especially compound consonants), and span very different character set sizes—from 6 Telugu vowels to 156 Tamil compounds.

Most published work addresses a single script in isolation [1,3,4]. Practical deployment, however, requires handling unknown-script input—a realistic scenario for multilingual document scanners.

Contributions:

  • Two-stage pipeline (router→classifier) for four Indic scripts.
  • Seven-axis ablation study on Devanagari.
  • Robustness benchmark under six synthetic corruptions.

2.  Related Work

Devanagari.

Acharya et al. [1] achieved 98.47% on UCI (36 classes). We extend to 46 classes, reaching 99.48%.

Tamil.

CNN baselines on uTHCD [2] report 95–97% on restricted subsets; our ScriptCNN reaches 97.30% across all 156 classes.

Bengali.

BanglaLekha-Isolated [3] baselines hover ~92%; we reach 93.02% with no pre-training.

Multi-script.

Paul et al. [7] require script labels at test time. Our router eliminates this requirement.

Lightweight CNNs.

MobileNetV2 [8] and EfficientNet [9] target mobile deployment. A vanilla 4-block CNN suffices at ~7 M parameters per classifier.

3.3  Preprocessing & Augmentation

Greyscale, resize to 64×64 px, normalise to [−1,1]. Training augmentations: rotation (±15°), affine shear (±5°), perspective distortion (0.2), Gaussian blur (σ∈[0.1,1.5]), random erasing (p=0.2).

4.  Method

4.1  Architecture

ScriptRouter — 3-block CNN → 2-layer MLP (dropout 0.5). ~1.5 M params. Converges to 99.92% in 13 epochs.

ScriptCNN — configurable n-block CNN (default n=4). Each block: Conv→BN→ReLU→Conv→BN→ReLU→MaxPool. Feature maps 32→256 across four blocks. Global average pool → linear head.

10.  Limitations

Tamil blur sensitivity. Accuracy collapses under light blur. Fix: add Gaussian blur to Tamil training augmentations.

Telugu dataset size. Only 900 training images; results may vary across seeds.

Single-script assumption. The router assumes one script per image; mixed-script documents are unsupported.

No transfer learning baseline. A fine-tuned ResNet-18 would quantify the accuracy gap from training from scratch.

11.  Conclusion

We presented a two-stage CNN pipeline for multi-script Indic handwriting recognition trained entirely from scratch. Our system achieves 99.92% routing accuracy and 93–99% character accuracy, with worst-case CPU latency of 10.21 ms. Augmentation is the single most critical design choice (+93.56 pp). Tamil blur fragility is the key open problem.
